Methodology

What this tool does

It collects open-source evidence about a business — from automated queries of public endpoints, from public datasets you import, and from evidence you enter by hand — and converts that evidence into a transparent, adjustable 0–100 confidence assessment with a full source log.

What this tool does not do

  • It does not prove a business is real or fraudulent. It estimates how strongly the open-source record supports operational legitimacy.
  • It does not access anything behind logins, paywalls, CAPTCHAs, or robots restrictions, and it does not bypass rate limits.
  • It does not collect or transmit sensitive identifiers such as full EINs.
  • It does not run hidden network calls, telemetry, or analytics. The Network Log is the complete record.

How external source checks work

Each connector is a small, documented module: source name, endpoint, query built from your intake fields, parser, reliability rating, and error handling. Browsers enforce Cross-Origin Resource Sharing (CORS); many government sites block direct browser requests. When a connector fails for any reason — CORS, timeout, rate limit, outage — it records a FAILED result in the source log and presents a direct link so you can run the same check by hand and enter the result as manual evidence. A failed check is treated as "unknown," never as evidence against the business.

How manual evidence entry works

A manual entry carries the same fields as an automated one — source, URL, retrieval time, summary, reliability class, and effect — plus your strength judgment. Manual entries are marked MANUAL in the log and reduce the automation-coverage sub-score, which the report discloses.

How scoring works

Nine categories carry weights summing to 100 (defaults: registration 20, licensing 15, physical presence 15, website/domain 10, contactability 10, reputation 10, procurement 10, identity consistency 10 — totaling 100; sanctions/exclusions findings act through red-flag penalties rather than category points). Within a category, supporting evidence earns credit scaled by strength (strong 1.0, moderate 0.6, weak 0.3) and by source class (official ×1.0, non-official ×0.7, analyst observation ×0.6, business self-claim ×0.2). Weakening evidence subtracts credit. Category credit is capped at 1.0 and multiplied by the category weight. Red-flag penalties are then subtracted, and the result is clamped to 0–100. Plain language: a single strong official source can nearly fill a category; a stack of self-claims cannot.

How source reliability is weighted, and why official sources count more

Government registries, licensing boards, and sanctions lists are maintained under legal duty, with identity controls and correction mechanisms. Directories, reviews, and social media can be created by anyone in minutes, including by the subject. The multipliers above encode that asymmetry. Self-claims by the business receive minimal credit because they are the thing being tested.

Why registration does not equal operational legitimacy

Registering an entity costs little and proves only that paperwork was filed. Shell companies, dormant entities, and hijacked registrations are all "registered." Registration is strong evidence the entity exists on paper; operating evidence — premises, contactability, customers, contracts, regulatory activity — must come from other categories. That is why registration carries 20 points, not 50.

Why one source is rarely enough

Any single source can be stale, spoofed, mismatched (wrong entity with a similar name), or wrong. Confidence comes from independent sources agreeing — which is exactly what the identity-consistency category measures. Treat single-source conclusions as unconfirmed.

How to interpret failed source checks

A failed check means "we could not look," not "it is not there." The tool scores it as missing evidence (lower completeness), never as a negative indicator — with one exception: if a failure prevents verifying a claim the business itself makes (a license, a federal contract), the analyst may raise the "external source errors prevent verification of key claims" flag, because the claim remains unsupported.

Limitations of open-source verification

  • Coverage is uneven across jurisdictions; many registries have no API and some have no online search.
  • Name matching is fuzzy; similar names can pull the wrong entity. Confirm identifiers before relying on a match.
  • Open sources lag reality; a dissolved business can look active for months.
  • Sophisticated fraud invests in exactly the signals this tool measures. A high score lowers, but never eliminates, risk.
  • Browser-based checks are constrained by CORS; automation coverage will rarely be 100%.

Recommended analyst workflow

  1. Enter business name and known identifiers.
  2. Run automated source checks.
  3. Review source results.
  4. Add manual evidence where automated checks fail.
  5. Resolve identity inconsistencies.
  6. Review red flags.
  7. Adjust scoring only if justified — and record the justification.
  8. Generate the final report.
  9. Export the case file and source log.
